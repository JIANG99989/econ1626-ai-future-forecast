2027-2030: Will artificial intelligence make the overall economy higher for different income groups, or will it make the overall wealth distribution more uneven? 
Argument: What I advocate and why it matters
The core argument of this paper is that AI is likely to make the overall economy higher by 2027-2030, but it may not necessarily make most income groups rich in equal proportion. Without policies for diffusion, competition, and redistribution, AI is more likely to widen wealth and income inequality. On the one hand, AI has increased productivity and reduced the cost of some services and created new products, but on the other hand, it may also concentrate more profits on capital owners and highly skilled workers, in the hands of large platform enterprises and organizations with data resources. 
Existing research generally agrees that generative AI has significant productivity potential. The McKinsey Global Institute estimates that generative AI could add trillions of dollars to the global economy each year, especially in customer service, marketing, and morein knowledge-intensive activities such as software engineering and R&D (McKinsey Global Institute, 2023). Goldman Sachs also predicts that generative AI could significantly increase global labor productivity and GDP levels over the next decade (Goldman Sachs, 2023). However, some studies suggest that macro returns may be lower than techno-optimists expect, as many tasks cannot be fully automated, and adoption and organizational adjustment take time (Acemoglu, 2024). 
Therefore, the author does not simply understand the question as "will AI replace jobs", but proposes a more specific economic judgment: AI will change the ability of different income groups to receive growth dividends. High-income groups often have financial assets, digital skills, and the ability to provide educational resources, making it easier to turn AI into revenue growth. Middle-income groups may have access to efficiency tools, but they also face pressure to restructure. Low-income groups may benefit from cheaper education, healthcare, finance, and public services. However, if there is a lack of training and protection, it may also bear higher employment risks. 
Under the baseline scenario, AI could increase GDP levels in major adopting economies by about 1%-3% from baseline by 2030, but the benefits will be unevenly distributed. In the revenue scenario, if AI spreads widely to SMEs, the public sector, and low-income groups, the overall GDP gain could reach 4%-7%. In a downward scenario, if AI mainly strengthens capital and platform monopolies, GDP gains may be less than 1.5%, but wealth concentration and income disparity will rise.
This question is important because AI is not a tool that only affects tech companies. The IMF estimates that approximately 40% of global employment is exposed to AI, while the exposure rate in advanced economies may approach 60% (IMF, 2024). This means that AI can be both a tool to help all income groups grow and a wealth concentration accelerator. It is not the technology itself that determines the direction, but the institutions, market structures and public policies. 

AI capabilities to the distribution of benefits to different income groups
The mechanism by which AI affects different income groups can be divided into five links: employment, ability, task, public service and assets. 

1. Employment Channels: AI changes the salary structure, not just the number of jobs
The impact of AI on employment depends on the scale effect and the substitution effect. The scale effect means that demand expands after costs fall, and enterprises create new jobs and services. The substitution effect means that companies use AI to reduce certain roles or tasks. 
By 2030, it is more likely that there will be not mass general unemployment, but a change in the wage structure. 
1. If highly skilled workers use AI, productivity and income may increase. 
2. Medium-skilled white-collar positions face task restructuring and wage pressure. 
3. Low-income workers may benefit from low-cost services, but the quality of employment may not improve. 
4. New positions are added, such as AI process and model design, data governance, industry automation consultants, and human-machine collaboration management. 
As a result, AI could create a new income gap between those who can use AI and those who cannot. Acemoglu believes that the macro benefits of AI should not be simply exaggerated, but rather on which tasks are actually effectively automated and whether automation creates new tasks that complement each other (Acemoglu, 2024).

2. Capability Channels: AI reduces the marginal cost of knowledge work
AI can complete text translation, generation, code writing, data analysis, image generation, and moreCustomer service response and process automation. The Stanford AI Index report shows that AI models' capabilities in multiple benchmarks continue to improve, while enterprise investment and adoption of generative AI are also rising rapidly (Stanford HAI, 2024). By 2027-2030, the more important ability may not be to answer a question once, but to be able to invoke tools, connect to databases, perform multi-step tasks, and complete automation step by step. 
This reduces the cost of many knowledge and service tasks. For example, a student can get personalized tutoring. A small business can use AI to generate marketing text, create financial summaries, and respond to customers. A lawyer can retrieve cases faster. Experimental studies have found that AI can significantly improve the speed and quality of completing some writing and knowledge tasks, especially for less experienced workers (Noy and Zhang, 2023). In customer service scenarios, AI-assisted tools have also been found to increase agent productivity and improve even more for less experienced employees (Brynjolfsson et al., 2023). 
But ability itself does not automatically lead to fairness. Whoever has high-quality models, high-quality data, large enough computing power, organizational ability and usage skills will be more likely to reap the benefits. （OECD, 2023）。
 
3. Task channels: Different income groups face different exposures
AI changes the task first, not the entire profession. Research by OpenAI and the University of Pennsylvania points out that large language models can affect some tasks in the profession, but occupational exposure does not mean that the occupation is completely replaced (Eloundou et al., 2023). This distinction is crucial because some tasks can be automated, others can only be enhanced, and others still rely on human judgment.

Jobs performed by high-income groups often involve a large number of cognitive tasks, such as analysis, writing, programming, management, and decision-making. Although these tasks are more exposed to AI, many of them are "enhanced" rather than "replaced". For example, senior engineers, consultants, financial analysts, and managers can use AI to amplify personal output efficiency. Studies have shown that AI has a significant efficiency improvement in professional writing and code assistance, but the ultimate benefit depends on whether workers can effectively embed the tool into the workflow (Noy and Zhang, 2023; Brynjolfsson et al., 2023）。 
In the middle-income group, tasks in accounting, customer service, sales support, primary design, basic copywriting, and some data processing roles are more likely to be automated. These jobs may not disappear completely, but the manpower required for unit output may be reduced and the bargaining power of wages may also be weakened. The IMF also pointed out that the impact of AI on employment in high-income economies is twofold: some workers will gain increased capabilities, while others will face the risk of being replaced (IMF, 2024). 

Many jobs in low-income groups still rely on offline services, manual labor, caregiving, catering, transportation, and local services, and may not be directly replaced by generative AI in the short term. However, once AI is combined with scheduling systems, automated customer service, and platform management, low-income workers may also face problems such as increased work intensity and reduced job quality (OECD, 2023).

4. Public Service Channels: AI also has the potential to be a tool to bridge the gap

AI may not exacerbate inequality. If governments and public institutions can use AI to improve education, healthcare, employment services, and social security, low-income groups may see significant improvements. For example:

Free or low-cost AI tutoring systems that help improve educational opportunities

AI-assisted medical triage and medical record management can improve the efficiency of primary care services and an AI-powered employment matching platform to help workers find suitable jobs faster, as well as AI translation and government assistants to lower the barrier to access to public services and AI tools for small businesses. Improve the business capacity of low-income entrepreneurs.

The OECD points out that the allocation effect of digital technologies is not just about the availability of the technology itself, it also depends on infrastructure, skill levels, institutional arrangements, and the capacity of public services (OECD, 2023). Therefore, the distribution results brought about by AI are not fixed. The key is whether AI is monopolized by a few platforms or widely integrated into public services and small and medium-sized enterprises.

5. Asset Channels: Wealth inequality may increase faster than wage inequality.

Income inequality comes from wages, profits, and transfers, while wealth inequality depends more on asset ownership. A key risk of AI is that if the benefits from productivity gains are primarily reflected in corporate profits and stock appreciation, those who hold assets will benefit more than those who rely solely on salary income.

High-income groups typically hold more stocks, funds, corporate equity and real estate. Once AI boosts the profits of large tech companies, chip companies, cloud service providers, and automation companies, high-income and high-wealth families can gain additional income through the capital market. A real risk in the AI era is that technological dividends are quickly capitalized through capital markets, while wage dividends are relatively slow.

This means that AI could lead to a peculiar outcome: wealth concentration is rising despite overall GDP growth and lower prices for consumer goods and services. That's why GDP or productivity alone isn't enough to judge the societal impact of AI.
