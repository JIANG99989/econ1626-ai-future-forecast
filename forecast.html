2027-2030: Will artificial intelligence make the overall economy higher for different income groups, or will it make the overall wealth distribution more uneven?

Argument: What I advocate and why it matters

The core argument of this paper is that AI is likely to make the overall economy higher by 2027-2030, but it may not necessarily make most income groups rich in equal proportion. Without policies for diffusion, competition, and redistribution, AI is more likely to widen wealth and income inequality. On the one hand, AI has increased productivity and reduced the cost of some services and created new products, but on the other hand, it may also concentrate more profits on capital owners and highly skilled workers, in the hands of large platform enterprises and organizations with data resources.

Existing research generally agrees that generative AI has significant productivity potential. The McKinsey Global Institute estimates that generative AI could add trillions of dollars to the global economy each year, especially in customer service, marketing, and more in knowledge-intensive activities such as software engineering and R&D (McKinsey Global Institute, 2023). Goldman Sachs also predicts that generative AI could significantly increase global labor productivity and GDP levels over the next decade (Goldman Sachs, 2023). However, some studies suggest that macro returns may be lower than techno-optimists expect, as many tasks cannot be fully automated, and adoption and organizational adjustment take time (Acemoglu, 2024).

Therefore, the author does not simply understand the question as "will AI replace jobs", but proposes a more specific economic judgment: AI will change the ability of different income groups to receive growth dividends. High-income groups often have financial assets, digital skills, and the ability to provide educational resources, making it easier to turn AI into revenue growth. Middle-income groups may have access to efficiency tools, but they also face pressure to restructure. Low-income groups may benefit from cheaper education, healthcare, finance, and public services. However, if there is a lack of training and protection, it may also bear higher employment risks.

Under the baseline scenario, AI could increase GDP levels in major adopting economies by about 1%-3% from baseline by 2030, but the benefits will be unevenly distributed. In the revenue scenario, if AI spreads widely to SMEs, the public sector, and low-income groups, the overall GDP gain could reach 4%-7%. In a downward scenario, if AI mainly strengthens capital and platform monopolies, GDP gains may be less than 1.5%, but wealth concentration and income disparity will rise.

This question is important because AI is not a tool that only affects tech companies. The IMF estimates that approximately 40% of global employment is exposed to AI, while the exposure rate in advanced economies may approach 60% (IMF, 2024). This means that AI can be both a tool to help all income groups grow and a wealth concentration accelerator. It is not the technology itself that determines the direction, but the institutions, market structures and public policies.

AI Capabilities and the Distribution of Benefits Across Different Income Groups

The mechanism by which AI affects different income groups can be divided into five links: employment, ability, task, public service, and assets.

1. Employment Channels: AI Changes the Salary Structure, Not Just the Number of Jobs

The impact of AI on employment depends on the scale effect and the substitution effect. The scale effect means that demand expands after costs fall, and enterprises create new jobs and services. The substitution effect means that companies use AI to reduce certain roles or tasks.

By 2030, it is more likely that there will be not mass general unemployment, but a change in the wage structure.

  1. If highly skilled workers use AI, productivity and income may increase.
  2. Medium-skilled white-collar positions face task restructuring and wage pressure.
  3. Low-income workers may benefit from low-cost services, but the quality of employment may not improve.
  4. New positions are added, such as AI process and model design, data governance, industry automation consultants, and human-machine collaboration management.

As a result, AI could create a new income gap between those who can use AI and those who cannot. Acemoglu believes that the macro benefits of AI should not be simply exaggerated, but should rather focus on which tasks are actually effectively automated and whether automation creates new tasks that complement each other (Acemoglu, 2024).
